# Artifact 4: The Derivation Bridge
## From Topological Risk to the Operational Triad
**February 2026**

---

## 1. What This Artifact Must Demonstrate

L1 describes risk as the geometry of state-space contraction: the reachable state manifold M(t), the perturbation field P, and the response dynamics R, characterized by five morphological invariants and four modes of contraction.

L2 — the operational layer — is Simon's triadic definition: risk as the dynamic relationship between the likelihood of an uncertain event (probability), the severity of its potential impact (impact), and the capacity of the system to contain or absorb that impact (containment).

This artifact must show that the triad is a **projection** of L1 geometry — that probability, impact, and containment emerge from the topological description as three natural views of the same underlying shape. If the derivation is forced, the stack has a seam. If it is natural, the triad gains geometric grounding and L1 gains operational expression.

After establishing the derivation, this artifact reintroduces Capital Morphology as the specific interpretation of L0/L1 for economic systems — not the foundation, but a domain layer that inherits its structure from the layers beneath.

---

## 2. The Nature of Projection

A projection maps a higher-dimensional structure onto a lower-dimensional space, preserving some features and collapsing others. The triad, as a projection of L1, is not a simplification or a lossy compression — it is a *view*. Each triadic dimension captures a specific aspect of the full risk topology while being silent about the others.

This means:

- No single triadic dimension contains the full risk shape. The three dimensions together approximate it, but the full L1 geometry is richer than any triadic representation.
- Different systems with the same triadic profile can have different L1 geometries. The triad is useful precisely because it collapses complexity into actionable dimensions — but the collapse is real and structural information is lost.
- The value of the triad is not that it replaces L1 but that it provides the operational surface through which L1 geometry becomes measurable, communicable, and actionable.

The derivation below traces each triadic dimension back to its geometric source in L1, showing what is preserved and what is collapsed in each projection.

## 3. Deriving Probability

### The triadic concept

Probability, in Simon's formulation, is the likelihood of an uncertain event — the realm of uncertainty. It captures the fact that the system's future is not determined and that some futures are more likely than others.

### The L1 source: Branching structure under perturbation

Probability projects from the interaction between M(t)'s branching structure and P's dynamics.

At L1, the system occupies a present state from which multiple trajectories extend into the future. These trajectories are not equally weighted — the perturbation field P biases the system toward some trajectories and away from others, depending on P's intensity, correlation structure, and coupling to the system.

Probability, as an operational concept, is the result of collapsing this full trajectory landscape into a single dimension: *how likely is it that the system follows a trajectory that produces contraction?*

This collapse involves several compressions:

**From trajectory space to event space.** L1 describes a continuous manifold of branching trajectories. Probability-as-operational-concept typically refers to discrete events ("a liquidity crisis," "a governance failure," "a fire"). The projection from trajectory space to event space is the act of defining which sets of trajectories count as "the event." This is a modeling choice, not a feature of L1 itself — L1 provides the trajectory landscape; the probability projection partitions it into event-classes.

**From perturbation field to likelihood.** P is a structured field with intensity, correlation, and coupling properties. Probability collapses this into a scalar: how probable is it that P pushes the system into a contracting trajectory? The scalar discards P's internal structure (its correlation geometry, its coupling specificity) while preserving its aggregate effect on trajectory selection.

**From branching density to uncertainty.** The density of branching in M(t) — how many trajectories emanate from the present state, how rapidly they diverge — is the geometric source of uncertainty. A system with dense, rapidly diverging branching (many possible futures, sensitive to perturbation) maps onto high uncertainty. A system with sparse, slowly diverging branching (few possible futures, insensitive to perturbation) maps onto low uncertainty.

### What the projection preserves

Probability preserves the *exposure dimension* of L1: how open is the system to perturbation-driven trajectory selection, and what is the likelihood that the selected trajectory involves contraction?

### What the projection collapses

Probability collapses the internal geometry of P (losing correlation structure and coupling specificity), the topology of M(t) (losing information about which *kinds* of contraction are likely, not just how likely contraction is in aggregate), and the response dynamics R (probability alone says nothing about what happens once a trajectory begins).

### Morphological invariants most relevant to probability

**Connectivity density** — determines how perturbation propagates through the system and therefore which trajectories become accessible under P. High connectivity with contagion structure amplifies the probability of correlated contraction events.

**Dependency concentration** — determines whether the likely contraction trajectories are concentrated around a few critical points (making specific events highly probable) or distributed across the manifold (making any specific event less probable but the aggregate probability of *some* contraction higher).

## 4. Deriving Impact

### The triadic concept

Impact, in Simon's formulation, is the severity of potential consequences — the degree of deformation if an uncertain event is realized.

### The L1 source: Manifold curvature and contraction geometry

Impact projects from the geometry of M(t) under deformation — specifically, from the relationship between the perturbation applied and the contraction produced.

At L1, impact is not a single number. It is a geometric property: the *shape and depth of manifold contraction* along the trajectory the system follows. A perturbation that produces shallow, broadly distributed volume contraction has a different geometric impact than one that produces deep dimensional collapse concentrated at a critical dependency. Both "reduce the system's reachable futures," but the geometry of that reduction — its depth, its mode, its reversibility — is structurally different.

The projection from this geometry to the operational concept of "impact" involves several compressions:

**From contraction geometry to severity scalar.** L1 distinguishes four modes of contraction (volume, dimensional collapse, topological fragmentation, curvature steepening), each with different structural significance. Impact-as-operational-concept typically collapses these into a single severity dimension: how bad is it? This collapse discards the mode information while preserving the aggregate magnitude.

**From manifold deformation to consequence.** At L1, contraction is a geometric fact — the manifold has a different shape than before. The operational concept of "impact" adds an interpretive layer: contraction of *what*, as experienced by *whom*, measured in *what units*? This is where the observer-relativity finding from the stress test (Artifact 3) enters the operational layer. Impact is always impact *relative to a chosen identity boundary and a chosen metric of consequence*. L1 provides the deformation; the impact projection requires a specification of what counts as severe.

**This is the point where normative content enters the framework.** L1 is ethically neutral — contraction is a geometric fact, not a judgment. Impact is the first triadic dimension that carries normative weight: some contractions matter more than others, and "mattering" is not a geometric property. The projection from L1 to impact is the act of layering a normative metric (severity, loss, damage) onto a geometric substrate (manifold deformation). The projection is natural in the sense that it tracks real geometric structure; it is non-trivial in the sense that it requires a normative choice that L1 alone does not provide.

### What the projection preserves

Impact preserves the *deformation dimension* of L1: how much does the manifold contract, how deeply, and in what structural sense?

### What the projection collapses

Impact collapses the trajectory information (losing how the system arrived at this contraction), the perturbation structure (losing what caused it), and the response dynamics (losing whether the system is in the process of recovering or cascading further). Impact is a snapshot of deformation depth, silent about process.

### Morphological invariants most relevant to impact

**Redundancy** — determines whether perturbation produces localized or systemic deformation. High redundancy buffers impact; low redundancy amplifies it. A system with no redundancy in a critical function experiences maximal impact from perturbation to that function.

**Dependency concentration** — determines the *criticality profile*: which perturbation targets produce disproportionate deformation? High dependency concentration means impact is highly uneven — most perturbations produce modest deformation, but perturbation to the concentrated dependency produces catastrophic contraction. This is the geometric source of fat-tailed impact distributions.

**Connectivity density** — determines whether deformation propagates or remains localized. Dense connectivity can either distribute impact (spreading deformation thinly across many components) or channel it (transmitting deformation along contagion pathways). The structure of connectivity, not just its density, determines which effect dominates.

## 5. Deriving Containment

### The triadic concept

Containment, in Simon's formulation, is the capacity of the system to absorb or contain disruption — the system's resilience.

### The L1 source: Response dynamics R

Containment projects from R — the full configuration of the system's response dynamics, including their modes, their speed, their coupling to the perturbation, and their dependence on the morphological invariants.

This is the most direct of the three derivations. Containment is, in operational terms, almost exactly what R describes in geometric terms: how the system responds when perturbation pushes it toward contraction. The projection is less a compression than a translation.

But "less a compression" is not "no compression." The projection still involves several mappings:

**From response mode spectrum to capacity scalar.** L1 distinguishes four response modes (restoring, absorbing, deforming, cascading). A real system's R is typically a mixture — some perturbations are absorbed, others produce deformation, still others trigger partial cascades that are then contained. Containment-as-operational-concept collapses this spectrum into a single dimension: how much disruption can the system handle before irreversible contraction occurs?

**From temporal dynamics to capacity.** R is inherently temporal — it unfolds over time. Feedback latency, response speed, and the interaction between immediate response and long-term recovery are all part of R's structure. Containment collapses this temporal richness into a more static concept: *capacity* to absorb. This is a significant compression — a system with strong but slow response dynamics has a different R than one with moderate but fast dynamics, but they might receive similar containment ratings if the temporal structure is not preserved.

**From invariant-dependent dynamics to aggregate resilience.** R's strength depends on multiple morphological invariants — redundancy provides backup pathways, connectivity density determines propagation geometry, feedback latency determines response speed, regeneration rate determines recovery from deformation. Containment collapses these into a composite: the system's overall capacity to prevent perturbation from producing permanent contraction.

### What the projection preserves

Containment preserves the *response dimension* of L1: how capable is the system of resisting, absorbing, adapting to, or recovering from perturbation-driven contraction?

### What the projection collapses

Containment collapses the specificity of R (which response modes are active, against which perturbation types) and the temporal structure of response (fast vs. slow, immediate vs. delayed, one-time vs. sustained). It also collapses the distinction between preventing contraction (restoring dynamics), transforming it (absorbing dynamics), and recovering from it (regeneration) — all of which contribute to "containment" but through structurally different mechanisms.

### Morphological invariants most relevant to containment

All five invariants contribute to containment, making it the most invariant-rich of the three triadic dimensions:

**Redundancy** — provides alternative pathways when primary ones are disrupted, enabling restoring dynamics.

**Feedback latency** — determines how quickly R engages. Short latency means the system begins responding before deformation deepens.

**Regeneration rate** — determines whether the system can recover manifold geometry after deformation, not just resist it during perturbation.

**Connectivity density** — determines whether response dynamics can propagate corrective signals across the system (distributed resilience) or are isolated to local regions.

**Dependency concentration** — determines whether the system's response capacity is itself concentrated on critical components (making R vulnerable to the same perturbation that triggers contraction) or distributed.

## 6. The Triad as Three Views of One Shape

The derivation reveals that the three triadic dimensions are not three independent measurements added together. They are three projections of a single underlying geometry — three views of the same risk shape, each capturing a different aspect and each silent about the others.

### What this means structurally

**The triad is incomplete by design.** Each projection collapses information the others preserve. Probability without impact is exposure without consequence. Impact without containment is deformation without response. Containment without probability is capacity without context. Only together do the three projections approximate the full risk shape — and even together, they lose information that L1 retains (contraction modes, temporal dynamics, invariant interactions).

**The triad is non-redundant.** Each projection captures geometric information the other two do not. This is not a coincidence — it reflects the structural independence of the three L1 sources. Branching structure (source of probability) is independent of deformation geometry (source of impact), which is independent of response dynamics (source of containment). The three aspects of risk topology are genuinely distinct, which is why the triad works as an operational decomposition.

**Aggregate scores destroy shape.** If the three projections are collapsed into a single weighted average, the result discards the very structure that makes the triad useful. A system with high probability, low impact, and high containment has a fundamentally different risk shape than one with low probability, high impact, and low containment — even if their weighted averages are identical. The first system faces frequent but absorbable disruptions; the second faces rare but potentially catastrophic ones. This is the geometric basis for the alignment check's insistence that combination logic must preserve shape: because the triad *is* a shape, and averaging destroys it.

### The triad's relationship to the morphological invariants

Each triadic dimension draws on multiple invariants, and each invariant contributes to multiple dimensions. The mapping is not one-to-one but many-to-many:

| Invariant | Probability (branching/exposure) | Impact (deformation) | Containment (response) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Redundancy | Moderate: redundancy can buffer perturbation transmission, reducing effective exposure | High: determines whether deformation is localized or systemic | High: provides alternative pathways for restoring dynamics |
| Connectivity density | High: determines perturbation propagation, shapes which trajectories are accessible | High: determines whether deformation propagates or remains local | High: determines whether response signals can propagate correctively |
| Feedback latency | Low: latency affects response, not exposure | Low: latency affects response timing, not deformation geometry | High: determines temporal depth of unchecked deformation |
| Regeneration rate | Low: regeneration is post-contraction, not pre-event | Moderate: high regeneration means deformation is more reversible | High: determines recovery capacity after deformation |
| Dependency concentration | High: concentrated dependencies create high-probability contraction pathways | High: concentrated dependencies create disproportionate deformation | High: concentrated response capacity creates fragile containment |

This many-to-many mapping is why the triad cannot be constructed by assigning each invariant to a single dimension. The invariants interact across all three projections. This has a direct engineering implication: subscores that measure a single invariant contribute to *all three* triadic dimensions, not just one. The subscore architecture must reflect this cross-dimensional contribution rather than forcing each subscore into a single triadic bucket.

## 7. Where Normative Content Enters

L0 is ethically neutral. L1 is ethically neutral. The projection from L1 to the operational triad is the first point where normative content becomes structurally necessary.

Specifically, it enters through the impact projection. At L1, manifold contraction is a geometric fact — the system has fewer reachable states. At L2, this becomes "severity of consequences" — which requires specifying: consequences *for whom*, measured *how*, and judged *by what standard*?

This is not a defect in the derivation. It is a feature. The normative choice is made explicit rather than hidden. When a risk rating assigns an impact score, it is implicitly making a claim about which aspects of manifold deformation matter and to whom. Making this explicit (via the derivation) allows the claim to be examined and contested — which is exactly what good risk methodology should enable.

Probability is less normatively loaded (trajectory likelihoods are relatively observer-independent once the identity boundary is fixed) but still requires the event-classification choice: which trajectory-sets count as "the event" being assessed?

Containment is the least normatively loaded of the three — the system's response dynamics are structural properties that can be characterized without specifying whose values are at stake. But "capacity to absorb disruption" still requires specifying: disruption *of what*, and absorption *to what end*? A system can absorb disruption in ways that preserve one function while sacrificing another. Which function counts as "absorbed" is a normative choice.

**The implication for subscore design:** Every subscore that claims to measure impact must be explicit about whose perspective and what consequence metric it adopts. Subscores that measure probability or containment have weaker but still present normative dependencies. Making these dependencies visible — rather than embedding them invisibly in methodology — is an alignment requirement that flows directly from the derivation.

## 8. Reintroducing Capital Morphology

With the derivation complete, Capital Morphology can now be precisely positioned in the stack.

### Capital Morphology is L0/L1 interpreted for economic systems

Capital Morphology's core insight — that capital is a pattern of reliable generativity, a configuration of relationships that produces future options — is the L0/L1 framework read through an economic lens:

| L0/L1 concept | Capital Morphology interpretation |
|---|---|
| Identity-preserving dynamical system | An economic entity (firm, protocol, network, market) with persistent structure |
| Branching future trajectories | The entity's capacity to produce differentiated future economic states — new products, new relationships, new markets, new adaptations. This is **generativity**. |
| Reachable state manifold M(t) | The entity's **option space** — the set of economic futures available to it |
| Perturbation field P | Market dynamics, competitive forces, regulatory changes, technological disruption, participant behavior |
| Response dynamics R | Governance, management, adaptive strategy, institutional learning. In Capital Morphology's language, this is **stewardship** — the active, ongoing care that maintains and renews the system's generative pattern. |
| Manifold contraction | **Disruption of generativity** — the narrowing of future economic options, the degradation of the relational configuration that produces value |
| Manifold integrity under perturbation | **Coherence** — the structural wholeness of the system, its resistance to deformation. A system with high coherence maintains its generative pattern under stress; one with low coherence deforms or fragments. |

### The Capital Morphology triad as a domain-specific projection

Capital Morphology's own triad — generativity, coherence, and stewardship — is now revealed as a domain-specific naming of the L1 primitives:

| Capital Morphology triad | L1 source | Operational triad (projection) |
|---|---|---|
| **Generativity** | Branching structure of M(t) — the system's capacity to produce differentiated futures | **Probability** — the openness of generativity creates both possibility and uncertainty |
| **Coherence** | Manifold geometry under perturbation — the structural integrity of M(t) | **Impact** — brittleness (low coherence) amplifies deformation; integrity (high coherence) limits it |
| **Stewardship** | Response dynamics R — the system's capacity to restore, absorb, and regenerate | **Containment** — stewardship is the active exercise of response dynamics, the adaptive care that prevents or reverses contraction |

This triple mapping — L1 primitives → Capital Morphology triad → operational triad — is the full derivation chain. Each step is a projection: L1 is the geometry; Capital Morphology names what the geometry means in economic systems; the operational triad projects it into measurable dimensions.

### What Capital Morphology gains from this positioning

**Precision.** Capital Morphology is no longer a standalone framework that must justify itself philosophically. It is the economic instantiation of a domain-general ontology. Its claims about generativity, coherence, and stewardship inherit their structural validity from L0/L1 — they are not metaphors but geometric properties of economic systems described in domain-appropriate language.

**Boundaries.** Capital Morphology applies to systems where the dominant dynamic is value-production — economic entities, financial networks, productive organizations. It does not apply (or applies only loosely) to systems where the dominant dynamic is something else (ecological succession, mechanical load-bearing, biological development). L0/L1 covers all of these; Capital Morphology covers the economic subset. This boundary was always implicit; the derivation makes it explicit.

**Non-circularity.** Capital Morphology previously had a potential circularity: capital is generativity, risk is disrupted generativity, so risk is disrupted capital. With L0/L1 beneath it, the circularity dissolves. Risk is state-space contraction (L1). In economic systems, state-space contraction manifests as disrupted generativity (Capital Morphology). Capital Morphology is an interpretation of L1, not a self-referential definition.

### What Capital Morphology contributes that L0/L1 alone does not

**Normative orientation.** L0/L1 is ethically neutral — it describes contraction without judging it. Capital Morphology introduces the normative claim that generativity is valuable, that stewardship is a responsibility, and that systems should be designed to sustain their capacity to produce future options. This normative content is essential for risk *rating* (as opposed to risk *description*) because rating requires a stance on what matters.

**Economic specificity.** The morphological invariants at L1 are domain-general (redundancy, connectivity density, feedback latency, regeneration rate, dependency concentration). Capital Morphology maps these onto domain-specific economic concepts: liquidity depth, protocol composability structure, governance response time, ecosystem development capacity, whale concentration. This mapping is what makes L1's abstract invariants measurable in the crypto context.

**Design language.** Capital Morphology provides the vocabulary for communicating risk shape to participants in economic systems — "this token's generative pattern is brittle" is more actionable than "this system's reachable state manifold has high curvature near concentrated dependencies." The derivation ensures that the accessible language tracks the precise geometry, rather than floating free of it.

## 9. The Complete Stack (Integrated View)

With the derivation bridge in place, the full risk ontology stack is:

### Layer 0 — Ontological Substrate
An identity-preserving dynamical system with branching future trajectories embedded in a perturbable field, where some trajectories contract the reachable state space.
*Domain-general. Ethically neutral. Framework-independent.*

### Layer 1 — Topological Risk
State-space contraction characterized by manifold geometry (M), perturbation dynamics (P), and response dynamics (R). Five morphological invariants define risk shape. Four contraction modes describe how manifolds deform.
*Domain-general. Ethically neutral. Measurable in principle.*

### Layer 2 — Operational Risk (The Triad)
Probability, impact, and containment as projections of L1 geometry. Normative content enters through the impact projection. The triad provides the operational surface for measurement and communication.
*Domain-general but normatively loaded. Measurable in practice.*

### Layer 3 — Capital Morphology
L0/L1 interpreted for economic systems. Generativity, coherence, and stewardship as domain-specific namings of branching capacity, manifold integrity, and response dynamics. Provides normative orientation, economic specificity, and design language.
*Domain-specific (economic systems). Normatively committed. Operational.*

### Layer 4 — Subscore Architecture
Engineering decisions constrained by L3, derived from L2, grounded in L1, resting on L0. Each subscore tracks a morphological invariant through a domain-specific indicator, contributing to one or more triadic dimensions.
*Domain-specific (crypto token networks). Fully operational. The subject of Artifact 5.*

Each layer depends only on the layers beneath it. No layer requires the layers above it to be coherent. The stack reads upward as progressive interpretation (from geometry to economics to engineering) and downward as progressive grounding (from engineering decisions to the geometric structures that justify them).

## 10. Derivation Assessment

### Does the triad fall out naturally from L1?

**Yes.** Each triadic dimension maps onto a distinct L1 source — branching structure, deformation geometry, response dynamics — and the mapping preserves the structural independence of those sources. The derivation does not require ad hoc assumptions or forced correspondences. The compressions involved (trajectory space to event space, contraction geometry to severity, response spectrum to capacity) are genuine projections that lose information in identifiable, documentable ways.

### Does Capital Morphology re-enter cleanly as an interpretation layer?

**Yes.** The triple mapping (L1 → Capital Morphology → operational triad) is consistent and non-circular. Capital Morphology gains precision and boundaries from the positioning; L0/L1 gains economic specificity and normative orientation. Neither layer is diminished by the relationship.

### What the derivation reveals that was not visible before

**The many-to-many relationship between invariants and triadic dimensions.** This has direct engineering consequences: subscores should not be forced into single triadic buckets, because the invariants they measure contribute across dimensions.

**The entry point of normative content.** The impact projection is where values enter the framework. Making this explicit transforms "impact assessment" from a seemingly objective measurement into a transparent normative choice — which is methodologically stronger, not weaker.

**The structural reason why averaging destroys information.** The three triadic dimensions are projections of independent geometric sources. Averaging across them collapses independent axes — the geometric equivalent of averaging a map's latitude and longitude into a single coordinate. The result is not a simpler position; it is no position at all.
